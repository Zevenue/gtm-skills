# Sequence Framework

Rules for follow-up emails, day spacing, angle rotation, and when to stop.

---

## Sequence structure

Every campaign is a 3-email sequence. Not 5. Not 7. Three.

| Email | Timing | Purpose | Max lines | Max words |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Email 1 | Day 1 | Open the conversation — Situation → Insight → Inquisition | 3 | 75 |
| Email 2 | Day 3-4 | Rotate angle — different proof point, PVP offer, or adjacent pain | 4 | 60 |
| Email 3 | Day 7-8 | Final angle — case study, resource, or clean breakup | 4 | 60 |

## Day spacing rules

- **Email 1 → Email 2:** 3-4 business days. Not sooner. Give them time to see Email 1.
- **Email 2 → Email 3:** 3-4 business days after Email 2.
- **After Email 3:** Stop. If they haven't replied to 3 well-targeted emails, more emails won't help — the signal or the angle was wrong.
- **Never send on Mondays before 10am or Fridays after 2pm.** Tuesday-Thursday mid-morning is the sweet spot.

## Angle rotation strategy

The goal of a sequence is NOT to repeat the same message louder. Each email should feel like a different conversation you might have at a dinner party — related but distinct.

### Email 1 → Email 2 rotation

| Email 1 pattern | Email 2 angle |
|---|---|
| PQS (pain-qualified) | PVP — do something useful for them, or share a different proof point |
| PVP (permissionless value) | PQS — now that you've given value, name the underlying pain |
| Pain-led (fallback) | Social proof — "talked to 3 companies like yours last month, here's what they said" |

### Email 2 → Email 3 rotation

Email 3 is always one of three things:
1. **Mini case study** (2-3 sentences max) — "[Similar company] had [same problem]. [What changed]. [Result]." Then ask if it's relevant.
2. **Resource offer** — "Put together [specific thing] for companies in [their space]. Want it?"
3. **Clean breakup** — "Seems like this isn't a priority right now — totally fair. If [trigger event] happens, I'm around."

Never use a guilt-trip breakup ("I've sent you 3 emails..."). That's manipulative and makes the sender look desperate.

## Threading rules

- **Email 2:** Can thread on Email 1 (same subject, reply-style) OR start a new subject line. Threading is better when Email 1's subject was strong.
- **Email 3:** Always a new thread with a new subject line. By Day 7-8, threading looks like pestering.

When threading:
- Don't add "Re:" manually — the sending tool handles that
- Keep the reply natural: "One more thing on this —" or just start the new angle directly
- Never reference that you "sent an email before" or "haven't heard back"

## Subject line rules (all emails)

- 2-5 words
- Lowercase (no title case, no ALL CAPS)
- No punctuation tricks (no "!!!", no "??" — one question mark is fine)
- No "Quick question", "Following up", "Checking in", "Touching base"
- The subject earns the open — it should create curiosity or reference something specific
- Good subjects reference: their tool, their role, their industry challenge, a specific metric

**Good examples:** `coordinator role`, `mindbody + eventbrite`, `lead time variability`, `pick error rates`, `post-raise hiring`

**Bad examples:** `Quick question`, `Following up`, `Great opportunity`, `Can we chat?`, `Intro - [Company]`

## When to stop a sequence

**Stop after Email 3.** No exceptions for the initial sequence.

**Re-engage only if:**
- A new signal appears (they posted a new job, raised funding, etc.) — this is a new sequence with new context, not a continuation
- 90+ days have passed AND you have a genuinely new angle
- They replied to any email in the sequence (positive or negative) — handle the reply, don't continue the sequence

**Never:**
- Send Email 4, 5, 6 in the same sequence
- "Bump" an email
- Send "Just making sure this didn't get buried"
- Re-send the same email with a different subject line

## Sequence-level QA

Before delivering a full sequence, verify:
1. Each email has a DIFFERENT angle (not the same pitch reworded)
2. The sequence tells a coherent story (Email 1's signal → Email 2's proof → Email 3's resolution)
3. No email references a previous email in the sequence ("as I mentioned...")
4. Subject lines across the sequence don't repeat patterns
5. Total sequence word count is under 195 words (75 + 60 + 60)
6. If threaded, the thread reads naturally; if not, each stands alone

## Handling replies

This skill writes the sequence — it doesn't handle replies. But for context on what good reply handling looks like:

- **Positive reply ("Yes, that's us"):** Drop the sequence. Respond personally. Ask one qualifying question, not three.
- **Neutral reply ("Tell me more"):** Drop the sequence. Send the resource/case study. One question.
- **Negative reply ("Not interested"):** Drop the sequence. Respond with grace: "Appreciate the honesty. If [trigger] changes, I'm around."
- **Objection ("We already have a solution"):** Drop the sequence. Acknowledge, ask what they're using, offer one specific comparison point. Don't push.
- **Out of office:** Pause the sequence. Resume when they're back. Don't send into a void.

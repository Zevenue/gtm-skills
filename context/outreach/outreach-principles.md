# Outreach Best Practices

## Core principles

### 1. You are not the star
The prospect woke up in a situation. Your job is to describe that situation so precisely that replying feels easier than ignoring. If your email starts with "We" or "I" or your company name, rewrite it.

### 2. The list IS the message
How you target determines what you say. If you need heavy personalization to make an email work, your targeting is wrong. A well-built list makes the copy almost write itself. Invest 80% of effort in who you're reaching and what signal qualifies them — the copy follows.

### 3. Problem before person
Personalization means understanding their condition, not knowing their bio. "I saw you went to Stanford" is trivia. "Most teams running Mindbody + Eventbrite end up with two payment flows" is personalization. Target conditions, not people.

### 4. Situation → Insight → Inquisition
This is the structure. Every Email 1 follows it:
- **Situation:** Name the prospect's reality based on what the data reveals
- **Insight:** One specific take that proves you've seen this problem before — something they'd only hear from someone who's worked on it
- **Inquisition:** Ask if you got it right. Earn a reply by asking for truth, not time

### 5. Earn replies, not meetings
Your CTA asks for truth, not time. "Is this you?" not "Can we schedule 30 minutes?" A reply is the win. The meeting comes from the conversation, not the cold email.

### 6. Every email is a hypothesis
Every reply is data. Every non-reply is data. Campaigns are experiments. Track signal → message → response rate and iterate. Don't fall in love with copy — fall in love with learning.

### 7. Three lines max
If you need paragraphs, you don't understand the situation well enough. If you need personalization tokens everywhere, your targeting is off. If you need a clever CTA, your insight is weak. Three lines. That's the constraint that forces clarity.

### 8. Data-first copy
The Clay enrichment pipeline gives you structured signals. Write copy against what the data reveals — don't go fishing for generic pain. If you have a job post signal, reference the job post. If you have tech stack data, name the tools. Let the data write the email.

## Two high-value campaign patterns

### PQS (Pain-Qualified Segment)
Target by pain intensity, not firmographics. The signal reveals specific pain → you describe the pain → you ask "Is this you?"

**When it works best:** High-intent signals (score 7+). Job posts, competitor usage with friction, public complaints.

**Structure:** Signal → specific pain → "Is this you?"

### PVP (Permissionless Value Prop)
Do the work before asking for anything. Find something specific and useful for the prospect. Deliver it. Then ask if they want more.

**When it works best:** When you have rich data about the prospect and can package it into something useful (audit, comparison, analysis, finding).

**Structure:** "I found this for you" → context on why it matters → "Want the full thing?"

## Operational principles

### Signal-based targeting over batch-and-blast
- Define what signals indicate buying intent for the specific client
- Build detection into Clay workflows
- Route signal-triggered leads differently — they get better copy, faster follow-up
- Batch leads without signals get the pain-led fallback

### Experiment cadence
- Every campaign is an experiment with a hypothesis
- Define success metrics before launching (reply rate, positive reply rate, meeting rate)
- Run for a sufficient sample size before drawing conclusions (minimum 200 sends per variant)
- A/B test ONE variable at a time (signal, subject, angle — not everything at once)
- Document learnings — what worked and WHY

### Copy iteration cycle
1. Write against the signal and the data
2. QA against hard rules (see Email Writer skill)
3. Launch to a test batch
4. Measure response within 5-7 days
5. If below threshold: change the angle, not just the words
6. If above threshold: scale the segment, don't touch the copy

## What to avoid

- **Generic pain:** "Growing companies face challenges" means nothing. Name the specific challenge.
- **Feature-leading:** "Our platform offers..." — nobody cares about your platform. They care about their problem.
- **Social proof as the opener:** "Companies like [logo] use us" — leads with you, not them.
- **Over-personalization:** "I see you love hiking and went to Cornell!" — creepy and irrelevant.
- **Vague CTAs:** "Would love to connect" / "Let me know if you'd like to chat" — connect about what? Be specific.
- **Long emails:** If it's over 75 words (Email 1) or 60 words (follow-ups), it's too long. Cut.
